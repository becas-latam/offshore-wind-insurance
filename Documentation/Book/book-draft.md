Insurance

Insurance as a concept has ancient origins, dating back to early human societies where mutual aid and communal support systems were common. The earliest known insurance practices can be traced to the Code of Hammurabi in Babylon around 1750 BC, which included provisions for merchants to receive loans to finance shipments with the understanding that the lender would cancel the loan if the shipment was lost or stolen. In ancient China, merchants spreading their cargo across multiple ships to reduce the risk of loss represents another form of early risk management. Similarly, in ancient Greece and Rome, maritime loans functioned as a form of insurance for sea voyages, where lenders absorbed the losses if ships were lost at sea. These early practices laid the groundwork for more formalized insurance systems, which evolved significantly in the 17th century with the establishment of the first insurance company, Lloyd's of London, which became synonymous with marine insurance. This marked the beginning of modern insurance practices, providing structured financial protection against various risks and uncertainties.

The origins of insurance in the offshore wind industry is based in the oil and gas sector. The insurance market has played a crucial role in the development of the oil and gas industry, as it is now also in the offshore wind industry, providing essential risk transfer solutions and financial protection for companies, shareholders, and the environment[2].

As the exploration and extraction of oil and gas involved significant financial investments and substantial risks, companies sought ways to mitigate potential losses. The development of specialized insurance products, such as Construction All Risk (CAR) and Operational All Risk (OAR) insurance, became essential. CAR insurance emerged to cover risks during the construction phase, protecting against physical damage and loss due to unforeseen events like natural disasters or accidents. Delay in Start-Up (DSU) is a specialized cover designed to mitigate the financial losses resulting from delays in project completion due to physical damage or other insured events during the construction or installation phase. OAR insurance cover operational risks once the projects up and running, including equipment breakdowns, production interruptions, and property damage.

Marine insurance policies were developed to protect against the loss or damage of ships, cargo, and liabilities during transit. Lloyd's of London played a significant role in pioneering marine insurance for the oil and gas industry, offering coverage for tankers and other vessels. For the vessels involved in the construction and operation of oil and gas projects, specific coverages like Hull and Machinery insurance protect against physical damage, while Protection and Indemnity (P&I) insurance covers third-party liabilities.

The offshore wind industry has adopted similar insurance programs to those used in the oil and gas sector. Offshore wind projects utilize Construction All Risk insurance

DSUdditionally, specialized coverages for vessels involved in offshore wind construction, such as installation vessels, mirror those used in the oil and gas industry. Once operational, wind farms typically employ Operational All Risk policies to protect against physical damage and business interruption.

Inter Array Cables

Inter-array cables connect individual turbines to each other and to the offshore substation. These cables enable the transmission of electricity generated by the turbines, ensuring it reaches the offshore substation for consolidation before being sent to the onshore grid.

Cable Structure:

Conductors: Typically made of copper or aluminium, these conductors are the core of the cable, responsible for carrying electrical current.

Insulation: Surrounding the conductors, insulation materials prevent electrical leakage and ensure safe operation.

Armor: Protective layers of steel or other materials provide mechanical protection against physical damage.

Optical Fibers: Often included for communication purposes, allowing for data transfer and control signals.

Interfaces with Foundations

Turbine Foundations: Inter-array cables are secured to turbine foundations using specialized cable entry systems. These systems prevent water ingress and mechanical damage, ensuring long-term reliability.

Substation Interfaces: At the substation, cables are routed through J-tubes or I-tubes. These tubes guide and protect the cables from external forces, ensuring a secure connection to the substation.

Statistics Cable Failures

Damage to these cables is a major concern, as it accounts for a substantial portion of insurance claims in the offshore wind industry. For instance, around 50% of claims related to offshore wind farms are due to subsea cable damage, with inter-array cables being particularly vulnerable during installation and operation. he average cost of repairing inter-array cable damage can range from $1.8 million to $12 million, and the downtime for such repairs can be extensive, averaging around 40 days.

CAR 3 (Construction All Risks - Leg 3): This type of insurance covers physical damage to the insured works due to defects in design, materials, or workmanship, as well as the cost of rectifying such defects. It offers broader coverage than Leg 2. For instance, if a cable is damaged due to a defect, Leg 3 would cover the cable's replacement, removal, and any associated costs, while Leg 2 might only cover consequential damages resulting from the cable failure. The contractor strongly favours Leg 3 coverage, considering it essential for managing the higher risk associated with array cable contracts.  Leg 3 is generally more cost-effective, as it allows for risk pooling and leverages the insurer's capacity to absorb potential losses.

CAR Leg 2 (Construction All Risks - LEG 2): This insurance type primarily covers consequential damages arising from insured events rather than the cost of rectifying the initial defect. In most of the cases that would  to not cover the accessing costs, meaning the costs of the vessel.

We, as Employer, would initially propose LEG 2 coverage, citing difficulties in securing LEG 3 coverage in the current insurance market, particularly for cable-related work. We could attribute this difficulty to the insurance market hardening, characterized by insurers' reluctance to offer extensive coverage for high-risk components like cables, due to past loss experiences and a desire to protect their profitability.

Leg 3 DIC (Difference in Conditions): This supplementary insurance policy bridges the coverage gap between Leg 2 and Leg 3. One option for the Contractor is to procuring a Leg 3 DIC policy as a potential solution if the employer cannot provide Leg 3 coverage. However, commercially the costs for this insurance will be passed to the Employer. In addition, this option introduces complexities by involving multiple insurers and potentially increasing administrative overhead.

Indemnity: As an alternative to insurance, the Employer normally can agree to financially compensate the other for specific losses or liabilities. In this context, the contractor could suggest that the Employer could provide an indemnity to cover the risk that would have been covered under a Leg 3 policy. However, the Employer is concerned about assuming the full risk of manufacturing defects, over which they have no direct control.

Extended Maintenance: This insurance covers damages occurring during a specific period after the project's completion. The sources indicate that the standard market practice is to offer extended maintenance for 24 months after the project is taken over. This period often does not align with the contractually agreed-upon warranty period, which can be longer.

Option 1: Employer Provides CAR Leg 3 Coverage

This option is preferred by contractors as it simplifies the process, reduces costs, and consolidates risk with one insurer. The employer acknowledges the availability of Leg 3 coverage for certain aspects of offshore wind projects and plans to obtain it. However, there are concerns about the availability and cost of Leg 3 coverage for cable-related work due to the insurance market's loss history and risk aversion.

Option 2: Employer Provides CAR Leg 2 Coverage, Contractor Obtains Separate Leg 3 DIC Policy

If the employer cannot secure Leg 3 coverage, the contractor proposes obtaining a separate Leg 3 Difference in Conditions (DIC) policy. This approach, while potentially more costly and complex due to multiple insurers, has seen some recent success. The employer has asked the contractor to quantify the risk differences between Leg 2 and Leg 3 coverage.

Option 3: Employer Provides CAR Leg 2 Coverage and Indemnity

Another alternative if Leg 3 coverage is not feasible is for the employer to provide CAR Leg 2 coverage and an indemnity to cover costs above a certain value. This self-insurance approach raises concerns for the employer about assuming the full risk of manufacturing defects, which they cannot control.

Option 4: Scope-Specific or Sub-Limited Leg 3 Coverage

This option involves obtaining Leg 3 coverage with sublimit for example 30 mio EUR. Challenges include aligning sub-caps with the overall contract liability and ensuring fair risk allocation between parties, particularly in a consortium.
